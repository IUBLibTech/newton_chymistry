Newton Sans Font

The Chymistry of Isaac Newton Project uses its own font, called 'Newton Sans', to present Newton's edited manuscripts online—complete with his alchemical symbols—without readers needing to install the font on their own equipment.

If interested, you are welcome to download the 2025 version of the open source 'Newton Sans' font for use in research and writing.

The 'Newton Sans' font was first offered on our site in 2010, but as work progressed new symbols were needed so we released updated versions in 2015 and again in 2025.

Collaboration with Unicode Consortium and creation of the Alchemy plane

In 2008, members of the Chymistry Project began to meet with the Technical Committee of the Unicode Consortium with goal of adding a new plane to the Unicode Standard for a large set of alchemical symbols in common use in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. Our Alchemy Unicode Proposal was officially adopted in 2009-10 and the alchemy plane was incorporated in Unicode 6.0 and later. The Unicode Alchemy plane, defined in cells 1F700-1F7FF, includes alchemical symbols that were in regular use in printed publications in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.

The 'Newton Sans' font covers all the symbols defined in the alchemy plane of the Unicode Standard, and for each alchemical symbol we used glyphs appropriate to Newton studies. But different alchemists used different symbols for the same substances, so we acknowledge that other scholarly projects may need to replace Newton's glyphs with glyphs more appropriate to the usages of other alchemical authors.

Other symbols in 'Newton Sans'

The symbols or special characters of particular interest in the 'Newton Sans' font can be seen on this Symbols page.

Unicode had already defined many symbols used by alchemists on other planes in the Standard. For example, some alchemical symbols already had more than one meaning when Newton and his colleagues were using them, like the astrological symbols which stood for both planets and elements. And he used standard symbols to mark units of weight still used today by pharmacists and jewellers. Unicode had assigned most of these familiar symbols to the Miscellaneous Symbols plane, 2600-26FF.

To correct his notes, Newton used recognizable editorial marks, and to write Latin, he used a small number of conventional Latin abbreviations to simplify regular suffixes and omissions, which have all been added to the font.

Finally, to publish Newton's alchemical writings fully and accurately, we needed to create many additional nonstandard alchemical symbols. Newton frequently modified familiar alchemical symbols to extend their meanings in context, but the modifications were unique to his own work and, so, were not added to the Alchemy plane.

Symbols not defined in the Alchemy plane or elsewhere in Unicode, were placed in the private use area (PUA), E000-EFFF.

Unicode planes represented in 'Newton Sans'

The 'Newton Sans' font inherited broad linguistic coverage from its source fonts (included in the ZIP file), so it includes non-Latin scripts like Greek, Hebrew, and Cyrillic, all of which Newton occasionally used in his notes, and Arabic, which he did not use but which is central to the history of alchemy.

The 'Newton Sans' font provides coverage in these Unicode blocks: Basic Latin; Latin-1 Supplement; Latin Extended-A; Latin Extended-B; IPA Extensons; Spacing Modifier Characters; Greek and Coptic; Cyrillic; Hebrew; Arabic; Latin Extended Additional; Greek Extended; General Punctuation; Superscripts and Subscripts; Currency Symbols; Letterlike Symbols; Number Forms; Arrows; Mathematical Operators; Miscellaneous Technical; Enclosed Alphanumerics; Box Drawing; Block Elements; Geometric Shapes; Miscellaneous Symbols; Dingbats; Private Use Area (PUA); Alphabetic Presentation Forms; and Alchemical Symbols.
